1. Wi-Fi 6, 6E, 7 vs Wi-Fi 5

Wi-Fi 5 (802.11ac):
- 5 GHz only
- MU-MIMO (downlink only)
- Max ~6.9 Gbps

Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax):
- 2.4 + 5 GHz
- OFDMA (multi-user efficiency)
- UL + DL MU-MIMO
- TWT (power saving)
- Max ~9.6 Gbps

Wi-Fi 6E:
- Same as Wi-Fi 6 + 6 GHz band
- More channels, less interference

Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be):
- 2.4 + 5 + 6 GHz
- 320 MHz channels
- 4096-QAM
- Multi-Link Operation (MLO)
- Extremely high throughput (~46 Gbps theoretical)

--------------------------------------------------

2. OFDMA in Wi-Fi 6
- Splits channel into smaller subchannels (RU – Resource Units)
- Multiple users transmit simultaneously
- Reduces latency and contention
- Improves efficiency in dense networks

--------------------------------------------------

3. Target Wake Time (TWT)
- Schedules when devices wake up to transmit/receive
- Devices sleep when not scheduled

Benefits:
- Saves battery (important for IoT)
- Reduces network congestion
- Improves efficiency

--------------------------------------------------

4. 6 GHz Band (Wi-Fi 6E)
- Large amount of new spectrum
- More non-overlapping channels
- No legacy device interference
- Higher speeds and lower latency

--------------------------------------------------

5. Wi-Fi 6 vs Wi-Fi 6E

Range:
- Wi-Fi 6 → Better (2.4/5 GHz)
- Wi-Fi 6E → Shorter (6 GHz)

Bandwidth:
- Wi-Fi 6E → Higher (more spectrum)

Interference:
- Wi-Fi 6 → Moderate
- Wi-Fi 6E → Very low

--------------------------------------------------

6. Wi-Fi 7 Innovations
- 320 MHz channel width
- 4096-QAM (higher data rate)
- Multi-Link Operation (MLO)
- Multi-RU allocation
- Lower latency and higher reliability

--------------------------------------------------

7. Multi-Link Operation (MLO)
- Uses multiple frequency bands/links at same time
- Device can transmit/receive over multiple links

Impact:
- Higher throughput
- Lower latency
- Better reliability (failover between links)

--------------------------------------------------

8. 802.11k and 802.11v

- 802.11k:
  Provides neighbor reports (AP list)
  Helps client choose better AP

- 802.11v:
  Network-assisted roaming
  AP suggests better AP to client

--------------------------------------------------

9. 802.11r (Fast BSS Transition)
- Enables fast roaming between APs
- Pre-authentication and key caching

Benefit:
- Seamless handoff
- No call drops (VoIP, video)

--------------------------------------------------

10. 802.11k/v/r Together

- 802.11k → Tells client nearby APs
- 802.11v → Guides client to best AP
- 802.11r → Speeds up authentication

Result:
- Fast, seamless roaming
- Better user experience in enterprise networks
